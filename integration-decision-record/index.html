What this is. The complete reasoning behind how Marlex's integrations are rebuilt and routed as TargetRecruit is replaced with a native Salesforce system — grounded in a read-only audit of the live production org, not estimates. The core idea in one sentence: every rebuilt integration ships behind a Marlex-owned interface with a direct connection first, and the integration platform — MuleSoft, now chosen — is adopted one edge at a time, by configuration, on its own schedule, so the go-live never waits for it.

Each page states the alternatives, the choice, how it fails back, and — because honest work carries them — its own open questions.

The whole argument on one screen

If you read nothing else, this is the shape of it.

  • The platform is chosen; the go-live doesn't depend on it. MuleSoft was selected after demonstrations, with licensing the one open tail. But because every edge ships behind a Marlex-owned interface with a direct connection first, the switch to the new recruitment system can happen whether or not the platform is licensed, built out, or being operated yet.

The premise it rests on

Why the order can be relaxed at all

The reason this is a sequence and not a countdown is the swappable-adapter pattern: every edge ships behind a Marlex-owned interface with a direct connection first, so routing it through the platform later is an additive change — a second implementation behind the same interface, chosen by a configuration record, no redeployment. That single decision is what lets the platform's own timeline — its licensing, its build-out, the team's capacity to operate it — sit off the critical path of the recruitment system's go-live. The order below is therefore driven by reuse and dependency, not by a deadline.

Decision 1 · Why an integration platform

Integration logic lives outside Salesforce, behind Marlex-owned contracts

Should the connective tissue between Salesforce and the outside world sit inside Salesforce as custom code, be wired system-to-system with no layer in between, or run on an external integration platform behind interfaces Marlex owns?

Grounded in the July 2026 production-org integration audit · 19 July 2026 · architecture proposed for review

The decision. The rebuild — replacing TargetRecruit with Marlex's own Salesforce build — has to decide where the work of integration lives. Three shapes were on the table: all of it inside Salesforce as Apex, with no external layer; point-to-point, each system wired straight to each other system with no layer coordinating them; or an API-led, three-layer architecture in which Salesforce only exposes and consumes clean interfaces, and the transformation and orchestration between systems runs on an external integration platform (middleware).

What we propose. The API-led three-layer architecture, with integration, transformation and orchestration logic in an external middleware behind Marlex-owned canonical contracts. Salesforce exposes and consumes application programming interfaces (APIs — the defined request points a system offers) and holds no cross-system plumbing. This is the premise the whole integration set rests on, and it is proposed — the architecture records that fix it are drafted for review, not yet ratified. The concrete platform that plays the middleware role is a separate decision; the swappable-adapter pattern is what lets us hold this stance without the platform's timeline blocking the cutover.

What "three layers" means

Three layers, each with one job, is the shape being proposed

"API-led" is a way of arranging integrations into three layers, each doing one thing. It is worth stating plainly, because the whole set assumes it. A system layer speaks each end system's own language — the enterprise resource planning system (NET4, the ERP that holds finance and placements), the job board, the e-signature service — and exposes each as a clean interface, hiding that system's quirks. A process layer orchestrates: it combines those system interfaces into a business flow and transforms data between shapes. An experience layer tailors an interface to a specific consumer — a portal, a partner. Salesforce sits at the edge of this picture: it calls in through these interfaces and exposes its own, and it never holds the plumbing that stitches systems together.

The point of the arrangement is not the diagram. It is that the wire between any two systems stops being a private, hand-built connection and becomes a reusable, named interface anyone can call — so the second consumer of the ERP, or the second job board, is a small addition rather than a fresh build.

Why the premise matters now

The rebuild is exactly the moment to fix this

The org being retired is a lesson in the cost of the wrong shape. The TargetRecruit managed package's deepest expense was never the wire — it was business logic that knew vendor specifics, so every swap became open-heart surgery on code. The one genuinely modern corner of the current org shows the alternative already working: the ERP client is a cleanly separated family where the business logic calls a Marlex-owned interface and has no idea what sits behind it. The rebuild is the moment to apply that shape to every edge, because the edges are being rebuilt anyway — building them behind clean contracts costs little more than building them hard-wired, and building them hard-wired re-plants exactly the coupling the rebuild exists to pay down.

Ownership is the load-bearing word

"Behind Marlex-owned canonical contracts" is the part that survives every vendor and platform change. A canonical contract is a Marlex-defined shape for a business concept — what a candidate, a job or an invoice looks like at the boundary — that outside systems and Salesforce both speak to, instead of speaking to each other's raw formats. Own the contract and the transport becomes a detail: direct today, on a platform tomorrow, swapped without touching business logic. Skip the contract and the transport is the coupling, and every change reopens it.

The three shapes

All-in-Apex, point-to-point, or API-led with middleware

All integration logic inside Salesforce (Apex), no external layerRejected

Every callout, transform, retry and orchestration written as Apex inside the org. Cheapest to start — there is no second platform to license or operate. But it makes Salesforce the integration hub for the whole group, loads cross-system plumbing into the recruitment org, and runs transformation and orchestration against Salesforce's transaction and processing limits, which were never meant to carry high-volume mediation. It also cannot be reused by another org: when Axxon (Marlex's separately run business, on its own Salesforce org) needs the same ERP feed, the logic would have to be rebuilt inside Axxon's org, not called. The very coupling being removed comes back in a new coat.

Point-to-point, each system wired to each other, no orchestration layerRejected

Wire each system straight to each other system. Cheapest per connection on the day it is built — and it grows into the tangle every integration programme regrets: many private, one-off connections, no single place to change a transform, no shared contract, and a re-point at cutover that has to be repeated connection by connection with no seam to switch behind. This is the shape the current org's package coupling already is; rebuilding into it would spend the rebuild's budget to arrive back where it started.

API-led three layers, middleware behind Marlex-owned contractsProposed

Salesforce exposes and consumes clean interfaces; integration, transformation and orchestration live in an external middleware behind canonical contracts Marlex owns. Each edge gets a named, reusable interface instead of a private wire; the recruitment org stops being the group's integration hub; and a second consumer — another org, another job board — is an addition, not a rebuild. The cost is real and named: a platform to license and operate, and the discipline of actually keeping the contracts clean. This is the proposed premise; it is deliberately paired with the swappable-adapter pattern so that the platform's readiness never gates a go-live.

Point-to-point and all-in-Apex are the same failure at different scales — no owned contract, so the transport is the coupling. The API-led shape is the only one of the three that puts a Marlex-owned seam between business logic and every outside system.

Rollback for this choice

How this decision fails back

This is an architecture stance, not a switch thrown on a weekend — so it does not "fail back" the way a data cutover does. What protects it is the swappable-adapter pattern: every edge ships behind its Marlex-owned contract with a direct connection as its first implementation, and moving it onto the middleware later is an additive change — a second implementation behind the same contract, selected by a configuration record, with no redeployment. So if the platform is not ready — not licensed, not operable, not proven for a class of edge — any single edge simply runs direct, and the go-live does not wait for it. The premise is safe to adopt precisely because adopting it commits nothing to a timeline: the contract is what is permanent, the transport is reversible in a setting.

Two edges are proposed to stay direct permanently — the native CV parser (a latency-sensitive, in-flow AI call with no shared contract to own) and the finance data feed — and the commercial data connectors are middleware-exempt by nature, since they ship a fixed Salesforce connector with nothing to place a layer in front of. None of that contradicts the stance: the rule is a contract per edge and a direct-first transport, not a middleware hop for every edge.

Residual risk & open items

What we still carry

  • The architecture is proposed, not ratified. The records that fix the API-led three-layer shape, the contract patterns, and the responsibilities split are drafted for review proposed. Until they are accepted, the premise the rest of this set builds on is a recommendation, not a settled constraint — treat every downstream page the same way.
  • The canonical-contract catalogue is not yet defined. The stance names owned contracts as the load-bearing idea, but the actual catalogue — the Marlex-defined shape for each business concept at the boundary — has still to be written and agreed. Owning contracts costs nothing on a slide and real discipline in delivery; that work is ahead of us, not behind.
  • The platform choice is a separate, partly open decision. The product that plays the middleware role — MuleSoft, selected 3 July 2026 — is its own page; its licensing is not yet closed, and whether a two-person IT team can operate it unaided is a live operating-model question. This decision is built to survive that being unresolved, but it does not resolve it.
  • Contract discipline is cultural, not structural. Nothing physically stops a hurried edge from skipping its contract and hard-wiring a connection; the seam only holds if a code-review rule and a living register keep it honest. One tolerated "temporary" bypass and the whole premise starts to rust.
  • Open: which capabilities genuinely need the process layer's orchestration versus a simple system-layer pass-through is not yet mapped edge by edge — over-orchestrating cheap edges is its own cost, and that judgement is made per edge on the pages that follow.

Decision 2 · Platform choice

MuleSoft is the middleware platform — licensing is the open tail

Which product carries the integration layer? Chosen after demos. What remains is the commercial close and the question of who runs it.

Grounded in a platform evaluation and demos · selected 3 July 2026 · 19 July 2026

The decision. The group needs one integration platform to carry the rebuilt edges over time — the layer between Salesforce and the outside world (the ERP, the job boards, e-signature, the customer data platform, and the rest). Five candidates were weighed: MuleSoft (the Anypoint platform), Azure Integration Services, Amazon Web Services (AWS), custom-written Java microservices, and two lower-touch platforms named but not deeply analysed (Boomi and Workato).

Why the platform choice is not the cutover's problem

The open tail does not block go-live

A platform decision with unfinished licensing would normally be a schedule risk. Here it is not, and the reason is deliberate design. Every rebuilt edge ships behind a Marlex-owned interface (a "port") with a direct connection first; the platform is adopted per edge, later, as a second implementation behind the same port, switched by a configuration record with no redeployment. That pattern — worked in full on the swappable adapter — means the cutover routes through direct adapters, so the platform being unlicensed at go-live changes nothing. In effect the platform decision stays reversible until real traffic actually routes through it. That is why this page can settle the product without waiting on the commercial close.

The platform is not a mandatory gateway

Nothing is required to pass through MuleSoft. Reporting and monitoring tools connect straight to Salesforce over a Connected App, and two edges are proposed to stay direct permanently (the CV parser and the finance data feed — see the stay-direct edges). The platform earns each edge on its own merits — reuse across countries or systems, or protocol mediation — rather than sitting in front of everything by policy.

Decision 3 · The swappable adapter

The port is what we own; the transport is a setting

How the rebuild ships every edge to the outside world without waiting on a platform decision that is still open — and gets its rollback for free in the bargain.

Grounded in the July 2026 production-org audit · pattern proposed for peer review · 19 July 2026

The decision. Two dates are out of step. The middleware platform is chosen — MuleSoft, selected 3 July 2026 decided — but its licensing is not closed, so no edge can safely be built to depend on it. The rebuild, meanwhile, has to touch every integration edge and cannot wait. The question this page settles is how a rebuilt edge connects to the outside world today without either (a) hard-wiring it to a transport we may replace, or (b) holding the whole rebuild hostage to a platform that isn't ready.

What we chose proposed. Every rebuilt edge ships behind a Marlex-owned interface — a "port". A direct adapter is the day-one implementation; a middleware adapter is added later behind the same port; and a runtime selector — a configuration record — chooses which one runs at runtime. Switching an edge from direct to middleware, or back, is a configuration change, not a redeployment. This one pattern is what decouples the cutover from the open platform decision — and, as a corollary, it is also the rebuild's rollback mechanism.

The principle

What makes an integration expensive to change is never the wire

The cost of changing an integration is almost never the network call itself. It is business logic knowing vendor specifics — a recruiter workflow that names an endpoint, hard-codes an authentication scheme, or assumes a particular payload shape. The retiring package's deepest cost was exactly that coupling, spread across hundreds of classes. The org's one genuinely modern corner proves the alternative already works: the ERP client (MA_Net4Client) is a cleanly separated family — the business logic calls a Marlex-owned interface and has no idea what sits behind it. The rebuild applies that shape to every edge.

A port is that interface: a small, stable, Marlex-authored contract that says what an edge does (publish this offer, fetch this document, parse this CV) and never how or where. An adapter is one concrete way of honouring the port — a direct call to the vendor today, a call routed through MuleSoft tomorrow. Once the port exists, "direct or via the platform?" stops being an architecture decision and becomes a configuration choice, reversible either way. That is the whole move.

For the developersThe port is an Apex interface (or a small set of them, one per edge capability), authored under the MA_ namespace and depended on by the domain/service layer. Each adapter is a class implementing that interface — a direct adapter that makes the callout itself (Named Credential, Remote Site) and, later, a middleware adapter that calls the MuleSoft system API for the same capability. The selector is a Custom Metadata record read at runtime (a factory returns the adapter the record names), so which adapter runs is a deployable-and-editable configuration value, not a code path chosen at compile time. The existing MA_Net4Client family is the reference shape to copy; nothing about the pattern requires the platform to exist before the direct adapter ships.

The corollary that pays for the cutover

A lane built before cutover survives by "re-point, not rebuild"

The pattern earns its keep twice. The first payoff is transport independence, above. The second is that an edge already behind a port does not have to be rebuilt at cutover — it only has to be re-pointed. Because the business logic addresses the port and the port addresses the data through an adapter, moving from the retiring TargetRecruit schema (AVTRRT__) to the native model (MA_*) is a change inside the adapter, not a rebuild of the edge. The ERP link is the living example: it is preserved through cutover untouched above the port, and only its reads of the old model re-point beneath it.

Re-point, not rebuild

Building the seam before cutover converts a rebuild into a re-point. The System API that a lane exposes keeps its address and its contract; underneath, the adapter is re-aimed from the AVTRRT__ schema at the MA_* model. The outside system sees no change, the business logic sees no change, and the cutover cost of that edge collapses from "rewrite it" to "change one adapter's target". This is the port discipline paying back the coupling the rebuild exists to pay down.

Alternatives considered

Three ways to sequence the edges — two of them rebuild twice

The rebuild has to reach a working integration surface eventually, and the platform will eventually carry the edges that earn the hop. The real question is the order of operations. Three sequences are possible; two of them make the same mistake in different places.

A · Refactor the Salesforce callouts on TargetRecruit first, then rebuildRejected

Clean up every edge on the old platform — put ports around them while they still talk to the AVTRRT__ model — and only then rebuild the model underneath. It sounds orderly, but it pays for the seam twice: once against the schema that is about to be deleted, and again after the rebuild changes what the adapters point at. Work invested in wrapping the dying model is thrown away when the model dies. A double rebuild.

B · Direct callouts now with no seam, then refactor to middleware after the rebuildRejected

Rebuild each edge as a hard-wired direct connection with no port, ship it, and come back later to lift every edge onto the platform. Cheapest per edge on the day it is written — and it re-plants the exact coupling the package removal is paying down. When the platform is ready, each edge is opened up again and its business logic surgically separated from its transport, one class at a time. The seam gets built either way; doing it after the fact means building the edge, then rebuilding it. A double rebuild, moved to the other end.

C · Ports with direct adapters now; a middleware adapter per edge later, via the selectorChosen

Build the seam once, where it is permanent — in the org, Marlex-owned. Ship a direct adapter behind each port on day one so the edge works immediately and the cutover has zero platform dependency. When the platform is licensed, operable and earning its hop for a class of edge, add a middleware adapter behind the same port and flip the selector. The edge is built once; the transport changes by configuration, never by rebuild. The direct adapter stays as a permanent fallback, which is where the rollback comes from.

Why A and B are the same mistake

Both A and B build an edge, then rebuild it — A because it wraps a schema that is about to be deleted, B because it hard-wires a transport that is about to be replaced. C builds the edge exactly once and lets configuration absorb both changes: the schema move becomes an adapter re-point, and the transport move becomes a selector flip. The seam is the thing that makes both future changes cheap, so the seam is what you build first.

The honest cost

What the seam actually costs to build

The chosen pattern is not free, and pretending it is would undermine the case for it. There are three real costs, and they are worth naming plainly:

  • The ports themselves: roughly 3–5 developer-days. Defining the interfaces, the selector, and the factory that reads it — a one-time foundation shared across every edge.
  • The fallback direct adapters: roughly 10–15 developer-days. Each edge needs its day-one direct implementation. This is not overhead the other options avoid — B builds these too, and A builds an equivalent — but it is real work that ships before any middleware exists.
  • Dual-adapter maintenance during coexistence. For any edge that has both a direct and a middleware adapter live at once, both must be kept working until one is retired. This is a genuine carrying cost for the period an edge runs on the platform while its direct adapter is held as the fallback — deliberate, and bounded by when the fallback is stood down.

Set against those costs is the thing the pattern buys: the cutover sheds its dependency on an open platform decision, every pre-built lane becomes a re-point rather than a rebuild, and the rollback is a configuration flip. The seam is cheap relative to a coupled go-live, and it is permanent — it does not get thrown away when the old system does.

Rollback for this choice

This pattern is the rollback mechanism

Most decisions in the record need a rollback plan bolted on. This one supplies its own. Because every edge sits behind a port with a runtime selector, a transport switch — direct to middleware, or middleware back to direct — is a two-way door: a configuration change, rehearsable and reversible in minutes, taken per edge, with no redeployment. A middleware adapter that misbehaves in production falls back to its direct adapter by flipping one configuration record. The direct adapter is kept for exactly this reason; it is not scaffolding to demolish once the platform is in.

The exception: commercial data connectors

This two-way door does not exist for the commercial data connectors — the bought sourcing and screening tools that ship a fixed Salesforce connector, log in through Salesforce's own authentication and read through its standard interface. There is no Marlex-owned port to swap behind them, so there is no adapter to re-point and no selector to flip. Their only lever is the Salesforce side — which data each is allowed to reach — and their rollback is the external owner re-pointing their own connector, on a signal and in a window that must be agreed before the fallback opens. The full handling of that class is on the commercial data connectors page.

The decision. InfoJobs — Spain's largest job board — talks to the recruitment system through three flows: it receives the vacancies we publish, it hands back candidate CVs on request, and it delivers every application a candidate submits. Why this edge, and not the others

The one integration that writes the objects being retired

A read-only census of the live production org measured every non-human caller against the objects the rebuild retires. The finding that makes this page matter: across the whole surface, the job board is the one edge that clearly writes the old model today and that Marlex controls on both sides of the wire. That is exactly why it gates the cutover. So the webhook's re-point and the core go-live are one event, rehearsed together — the write edge cannot lead or lag the model it writes into.

What the edge actually is

Three flows, one of which writes

"The InfoJobs integration" is really three separate flows, and only one of them touches the write path that gates the cutover. Keeping them apart is what lets the harmless parts move early and forces only the webhook to wait for the core.

When a recruiter publishes a vacancy, Salesforce calls out to InfoJobs to post it. When a candidate applies, the system fetches their CV back out from InfoJobs over a plain web request. So this flow can move behind the lane early, but the parser it feeds cannot retire until the job-board cutover.

Every application a candidate submits arrives in through a public web service and creates records in the old model — the job, the application and the candidate objects. This is the write-coupled flow. It runs through a guest-user public site, is verified by a signed digest, and is the only one of the three that has to re-point in step with the core go-live.

Why the webhook gates the cutover

A write edge cannot lead or lag the model it writes into

The core go-live moves the candidate, job and application backbone from the old objects to the new ones in a single rehearsed window. The application webhook writes exactly those objects. So there are only two ways the webhook and the core can be timed, and only one is safe:

  • In lockstep with the core — chosen. The moment the core's applications live on the new model, the webhook writes the new model too. No application is orphaned; nothing lands in a retired object. This is the only timing that keeps the ~27,000-a-week inbound flow intact across the switch.
  • Out of step — impossible to make safe. If the webhook re-points before the core, it writes new objects that the live desk cannot yet see. If it re-points after, every application submitted in the gap lands in a dead model and is lost. Either way the busiest inbound flow in the system silently breaks during the one week the desk can least afford it.
This is what "gates" means, precisely

The webhook does not delay the cutover — it is bound to it. The core cannot go live until the webhook is ready to re-point in the same window, and the webhook re-point is only valid once the core has moved. They are one atomic event on the runbook, rehearsed as one. Every other Salesforce-owned edge is either already on the new model or read-only and forgiving; this is the single edge whose timing is welded to the core's.

The middleware lane

The second lane — heavier than the ERP, and it must land before the portals

Under the port rule set on the swappable adapter page, the job board ships behind a Marlex-owned interface with a direct connection first, and moves behind MuleSoft on its own schedule. It is the heavier build

Sitting at roughly 140 hours, it is the largest of the first two lanes — more than the ERP's ~105 hours — so it is built second, once the shared foundation the ERP lane establishes is in place.

The candidate, recruiting and staffing portals expose the same application pipe to the outside world. The portals cannot be built until the job-board pipe they sit on top of exists and is proven, so the job board lane lands before them. Building the lane and re-pointing the webhook are not two jobs. The same work that stands the job board up behind MuleSoft is the work that aims its write path at the new model. The decision. Decision 11 · Commercial data connectors

These connectors are exempt from the platform — for a product reason, not a preference

Three bought SaaS tools read the recruitment data through a fixed Salesforce connector. Can MuleSoft, the chosen integration platform, front them, and if not, who rolls them back?

Grounded in the live production-org measured surface · 19 July 2026

The decision. Three bought tools — Taleva (candidate sourcing), Orbio (an artificial-intelligence candidate pre-screening service) and Rainmakers (a business-to-business lead marketplace) — sit together as a class because a bought SaaS tool typically connects with a fixed vendor connector: it logs in through Salesforce's own sign-in and reads through its standard interface, with no setting to aim the connector at a Marlex-owned address instead. The question is whether the "route it behind the integration platform later" option — the rule that governs every Marlex-built edge — applies to tools like these.

What we chose. Where a tool connects this way, the rule does not apply and cannot be made to. There is no Marlex-owned port to place in front of a packaged connector, and MuleSoft cannot impersonate Salesforce to it, so a fixed-connector tool is exempt from the platform hop. Its only lever is on the Salesforce side — which data it is permitted to reach — and its rollback is the one integration risk on the whole programme that Marlex does not control: the external owner, not Marlex, must re-point the connector back to the old objects if the cutover is reversed. Taleva and Rainmakers are fixed-connector tools of exactly this kind. Orbio is treated the same way provisionally — but how Orbio actually connects is not yet confirmed, and if it turns out to reach Salesforce through custom interface operations rather than a fixed connector, it belongs with the Marlex-built edges instead. That open question is settled below, not assumed away.

Why the platform rule stops here

You cannot put a Marlex port in front of a packaged connector

Every Marlex-built integration has a Marlex-owned interface — a "port" — in front of it. That is what lets a rebuilt edge move behind MuleSoft later, on its own schedule, without the outside system ever noticing: the swap from old objects to new happens inside a layer Marlex owns. That whole mechanism depends on there being something Marlex controls between the outside system and the data.

A bought SaaS connector removes exactly that thing. Taleva and Rainmakers are commercial products that ship their own Salesforce connector, and Orbio is provisionally treated as one pending confirmation (see the open question at the foot of this page). A fixed-connector tool signs in through Salesforce's native authentication and reads and writes through Salesforce's own standard interface — the same interface any logged-in tool uses. Inside such a product there is no field to type a middleware address into; the connection target is Salesforce, and that is fixed by the vendor. So two things follow, and both are product facts rather than choices:

  • There is no port to route them behind. The connection terminates at Salesforce directly. Marlex owns no seam in that path to re-point, because the vendor's connector never touches a Marlex-built endpoint.
  • MuleSoft cannot stand in for Salesforce. Even if Marlex wanted to insert MuleSoft in the middle, it cannot impersonate Salesforce's authentication and standard interface to a packaged connector that expects to talk to Salesforce itself. There is no way to fool a product into calling middleware when its connector is built to call Salesforce.
So the one lever that remains is the Salesforce side

Because the connection cannot be intercepted or moved, the only control Marlex holds over these connectors is which data each one is allowed to reach — the object and field permissions granted to the account the connector signs in as. That is the whole toolkit: not routing, not mediation, just scope. It is why least-privilege scoping (a connector reaching only the objects it needs) matters more here than anywhere else on the integration surface.

Grounded in a read-only census of the live production org · 19 July 2026

The decision. The TargetRecruit managed package ships its own integration connectors — CV parsing, several job boards, e-signature and a scatter of utilities. What makes these different

Invisible until uninstall, gone the moment it happens

That is why they read as a consequence of the rebuild rather than an integration to migrate. The work is not moving them; it is deciding, ahead of time, which capabilities Marlex still wants and building native replacements for those — so that when the package leaves, nothing the business relies on leaves with it unexpectedly.

The trap: these do not announce themselves

Because a bundled connector holds no formal reference from Marlex's own metadata, it will never appear on a list of things blocking the uninstall. It passes every check and then vanishes. Rollback for this choice

For the Marlex-built edges, rollback means pointing an interface back at the old model. There is no equivalent here, and that is the defining fact of this page. After it, these bundled edges are deleted and cannot be brought back — the door only opens one way.

So the safeguard is entirely about sequencing before the door closes, not about a switch to flip after. Two conditions must hold at the moment of uninstall: the KEEP/DROP checklist is complete, with every capability decided rather than left implicit; and every capability marked KEEP has a live native replacement already running in production. If either is not true, the uninstall waits. Because it is the last step of the programme — held to the very end, after the new model is proven and the rollback window on the core cutover has closed — there is room to hold it until both conditions are met.

Decision 13 · The long tail

The small connections: confirm the dead, clean the rest, keep the tools

Everything that is neither a lane, a stay-direct edge, a native carry-over, nor bundled inside the vendor package — the dormant apps, the email services, the vestigial leftovers, and the tooling that stays. What happens to each.

Grounded in a read-only census of live production API traffic and login history · 19 July 2026

The decision. The census that measured the live production org sorts the integration surface into a handful of clean groups — the middleware lanes, the edges proposed to stay direct, the native carry-over edges, and the connections bundled inside the retiring vendor package. This page is the remainder: the small, miscellaneous connections that fit none of those buckets. Left uncatalogued, they are exactly the sort of thing that gets forgotten until the cutover trips over it.

What we chose. The tail is mostly confirm-and-clean, not build: drop the connections that measure as dead once their owners confirm it, re-point one email service to the new model, rename one mislabelled permission set, and keep the two tools that earn their place — the security-log shipper and the recruiter sourcing extension. None of it gates the go-live. The only real work is operational: getting an owner to say, for each dormant app, whether it is truly dead before it is switched off.

Why the tail exists at all

A census turns up leftovers, and leftovers need a verdict

A read-only sweep of the org's own usage records is thorough by design — it is meant to find every caller, not just the obvious ones. The price of that thoroughness is a residue: apps created for a one-off need and never switched off, email addresses wired to a process that has since changed, remote sites left behind by a tool that has moved on, and permission sets whose labels no longer match what they grant. None of these is a lane or an edge worth its own decision page. But each needs a verdict — keep, re-point, rename, or drop — because an unlabelled leftover at cutover is a small risk that costs nothing to close now and a debugging afternoon to close later.

The one that reads like a coupling but is not

DocsMadeEasy touches the schema, never the records

DocsMadeEasy — the document-merge product actually granted by the mislabelled permission set above (also known as HIC DocMerge) — looks at first glance like it might be coupled to the retiring recruitment model, because it works with object and field structure. It is not. The census measured what kind of call it makes, and every one of them is a describe call — a request that asks the platform "what fields does this object have?" to build its merge templates. It never reads a record and never writes one.

That distinction is the whole verdict. A tool that reads or writes the retiring objects has to be re-pointed at cutover; a tool that only inspects the shape of the data model simply sees the new objects once they exist. So DocsMadeEasy has no data coupling to move — the only work is to re-map its merge templates onto the new objects and fields, a configuration task inside the product, not an integration change. It is a managed package in its own right, distinct from the connections bundled inside the retiring vendor package, which disappear when that package is uninstalled — DocsMadeEasy stays.

A caution that runs through the whole census

Logins are a misleading proxy for how live a connection is

It is tempting to rank these connections by how often they log in — a busy login count reading as "important", a zero as "dead". The census shows that is unreliable in both directions, so no verdict on this page rests on the login number alone.

Why the login count deceives

The ERP link (NET4) logs in around 33,000 times in a month but makes only about 7,000 actual calls — it signs in constantly and does modest work. The customer data platform (Data Cloud) is the reverse: it barely logs in, yet it is the heaviest single caller in the entire census. Login count and real activity simply do not track each other. So liveness and relevance are judged by the integration user and the measured calls that user makes — not by how many times a connection signed in. A login figure is used here only as a rough presence signal (a genuine zero, like OIQ_Integration's, is still a strong hint of a dead app), never as the measure of how much a live connection matters. The full attribution is on the measured-surface page.

Where the evidence runs out

What the logs cannot tell us

The census is complete on evidence: it can prove that OIQ_Integration has made no logins since 2024 and that the CPQ app supports a capability the org does not use. What it cannot do is supply the last mile of each verdict — ownership and intent. The logs show that a connection is silent; they cannot say whether it is silent because it is dead or because it fires once a year for a process no one has run yet this cycle. Only an owner can close that gap.

So the drops on this page are drop candidates, confirmed by a person, not by a query. That is deliberate: switching off an integration user or deleting an app is cheap to do and awkward to undo if it turns out something quiet still needed it. The safe order is to record the counted fact — zero logins, unused capability — and hand each one to Marlex IT to confirm the owner and the intent before anything is removed.

Rollback for this choice

How this decision fails back

Rollback here is negligible, because none of the tail gates the cutover. These are cleanup items, not go-live actions. Dropping a dead app, retiring a vestigial remote site, and renaming a mislabelled permission set change nothing about whether the core can cross from the old model to the new — they can be done before or after the window, and reversing any of them is a matter of recreating a small piece of configuration, not replaying data.

The one item with any cutover coupling is the ProcessJobApplicantEmail service, which writes the retiring model today and re-points with the core. Its rollback is the same as any other Marlex-owned re-point covered on the adapter page: it is our switch to flip, so if the wider cutover reverses, this service points back at the old objects with it. Everything else on this page is untethered from the go-live entirely.

Residual risk & open items

What we still carry

  • Risk — a "dead" app is dropped while something quiet still needs it. A zero-login app could serve a rare, scheduled process rather than nothing at all. Mitigated by never dropping on the query alone: each drop waits for an owner to confirm the app is genuinely dead before it is switched off.
  • Risk — the mislabelled permission set misleads an administrator. A permission set labelled "DocsMadeEasy" that actually grants HIC DocMerge invites a wrong grant or a wrong revoke. Low-impact but real; closed by the rename, which is a label change with no functional effect.
