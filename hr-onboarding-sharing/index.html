Onboarding the internal HR team — and the private access model it forces

Bringing Marlex's internal HR team onto the recruitment platform to run internal recruitment, and refactoring record-level access so their confidential work is private — one initiative, because the two cannot be separated. This record captures the decisions we can make now and is honest about what still depends on HR's requirements.

Solution decision record · draft for peer review · 16 July 2026

What this is. The technical companion to the SHARE initiative — the reasoning behind how the internal HR / talent-acquisition team is stood up on the new native ATS, and how record-level access is refactored so internal processes stay confidential while external hiring keeps working exactly as it does today. It is a proposed solution for peer review, not a settled plan; the HR team's detailed requirements are still being gathered.

The core idea in one sentence. Mark every recruitment record as internal or external at the data layer, keep external records open to the whole recruitment population as they are now, make internal records private and roll them up a net-new internal role hierarchy so HR sees all internal work and a manager sees only their own line — all built on the new custom model, never on the packaged product being retired.

Why it is its own record. This is not part of the ATS rebuild's reveal — the rebuild replaces the platform; this onboards a new team and refactors access on top of it. It shares one decision with the rebuild (the access model at cutover), which lives on the rebuild record (open it and go to its Security & sharing page) rather than being restated here. Everything else here is specific to HR onboarding.

The premise

What the internal HR team asked for

Marlex runs recruitment for external clients on the ATS. The internal HR team wants the same platform for internal recruitment — with one hard constraint: their processes are confidential and cannot be visible to the wider business.

Raised in the ATS-rebuild coordination meeting · 16 July 2026

The ask. Bring the internal HR / talent-acquisition team onto the ATS to run internal recruitment — backfills, internal moves, onboarding — instead of third-party or home-grown tools. Today they have no system for it; the recruitment platform already does exactly this shape of work for external clients.

The constraint that shapes everything. Internal processes are confidential. The current org is open — everyone sees everything — which is fine for external hiring but unacceptable the moment a confidential internal process exists. So onboarding HR is inseparable from making the model private.

Why confidentiality is not optional

The director-replacing-a-director case

The clearest example, raised directly in the meeting: a regional director needs to open a process to replace an area director. If that process is visible the way external processes are today, the area director sees that their own role is being back-filled — that their job is at risk. The whole point of bringing HR onto the platform is defeated if the platform leaks the process to the person it concerns.

So internal work needs three things the open model cannot give:

  • Privacy by default — an internal process is closed, not visible to the whole recruitment population.
  • A hierarchical view — HR sees all internal processes; a regional director sees only their own and those of the people below them; nobody sees upward.
  • No public exposure — internal openings are never published to the external job-board / offers portal; they live entirely on the internal side.

What stays the same

External hiring must not change

The requirement is additive, not a redesign of what works. Every external candidate and job must keep behaving exactly as today — visible to and editable by the whole recruitment population. Whatever we do for internal confidentiality must leave the external world untouched, including for every record that already exists. That "don't break what works" constraint is what drives the flag-based design on the access page.

The confidential set, as described so far

The records HR flagged as sensitive are the internal processes themselves plus HR-owned data: salaries, interview notes, clock-ins, and internal-candidate applications. This list is indicative — pinning down exactly which fields and objects are confidential is an open question for the HR team, not something to assume.

Two framings the meeting surfaced

Not just sharing — and not necessarily now

Two points from the discussion shape the rest of this record:

It is a build

Onboarding a team is more than flipping sharing. HR needs their own kind of record, a management structure, their own access profile, and possibly onboarding-specific screens — all things that get built. That is why this is the "HR rollout and sharing refactor", covered on what we build.

Timing is a business call

The meeting explicitly did not settle whether this is urgent enough to do now or can wait for the rebuild. It was framed as "the least important of the important things, but the most important of the least important", and the People lead was named as the person who should decide urgency — see when it lands.

Next: the role hierarchy that exists today, and why it has no place for HR yet. The access model this requirement forces is on the rebuild record's Security page.

The starting point

Today's role hierarchy — geographic, with no HR branch

The org has a single 190-role tree organised by geography. It has two parallel branches under each region — sales and recruiting — and no branch for a corporate HR function. That gap is exactly why the internal role tree is cheap to build fresh.

Read from the live production role metadata · July 2026

What exists. One role hierarchy, 190 roles, rooted at a single node, structured by geography: region → area → office. Under each regional director sit two parallel branches — a commercial/sales branch and a recruiting branch. Every role grants edit on the core objects, consistent with today's open model.

What's missing. There is no internal-HR branch, because HR is a corporate function, not a recruitment office. The internal hierarchy the confidentiality model needs does not exist yet — and because there are no internal HR users to migrate, it is built from scratch, which is the easy case.

The shape

Region → area → office, in two parallel branches

190roles in a single tree, rooted at one top node
~10regional director roles, each splitting into a sales and a recruiting branch
0roles for an internal-HR function today — the branch does not exist

Reconstructed from the parent-role links, the tree looks like this:

The live tree (role API names) Marlex (root)
└─ executive layer — Managing_Director_Executive / [named-director role] / Director_de_negocio
   └─ Regional_Sales_Director_[REGION] — ~10 (MADRID, BARCELONA, GIRONA, LEVANTE, NORTE, SUR, CAT_CENTRE, CAT_SUD, ARAGON, GLOBAL_SECTORS…)
      ├─ Business_Area_Manager_[AREA] — ~19, the sales / commercial branch
      │   └─ MARLEX_[OFFICE] — ~71 office roles
      └─ Recruiting_Area_Manager_[AREA] — ~19, the recruiting branch
         └─ Recruiting_[OFFICE] — ~56 office roles

Two things matter for the HR design:

  • It is geographic, not functional. Access rolls up by where you sit — office to area to region — not by what function you perform. HR is a function that cuts across geography, so it does not fit anywhere in this tree.
  • The recruiting branch is the closest analogue. The internal HR branch will look structurally like the recruiting branch (a manager over a team, rolling up), but it is a separate sub-tree for internal work, not a re-use of the existing recruiting roles.

Why the gap helps

No internal users today means no migration

The absence of an HR branch is not a problem to solve — it is what makes the build cheap. Standing up the internal hierarchy is net-new: new roles, new users, new licences, with nothing to migrate and no existing behaviour to preserve on the internal side. Contrast the external side, where 190 roles and hundreds of users already depend on the open model and must be left exactly as they are. The risk in this whole initiative sits on the external side (don't break what works), not on building the internal branch.

Under an open model, the hierarchy is inert for visibility

Role hierarchy only grants access on top of a private baseline. Today the objects are open (everyone sees everything), so the hierarchy does nothing for record visibility — it is carried for reporting and forecasting. The moment internal records become private, the hierarchy starts doing real work: it is what gives a manager sight of their team's internal processes and no one else's. That coupling is the design on the next page.

Decision 1 · Internal vs external access

How internal work stays private while external hiring stays open

The platform must do two opposite things at once. The design: mark every record internal or external at the data layer, drive sharing off that mark, keep external open to everyone, make internal private and hierarchical — and make the periphery records inherit the model.

Solution decision · draft for peer review · 16 July 2026

What this decides. The record-level access design that lets external candidates and jobs stay visible to the whole recruitment population exactly as today, while internal HR processes are private and seen only up their own management line.

What we chose. Set the object default to private, then share external records back to everyone with a criteria-based rule keyed on a data-layer flag (a record type plus an Is_External__c boolean). Internal records match no such rule, so they stay private and are visible only to their owner, the HR team, and — via the net-new internal role hierarchy — the managers above the owner. Every record that exists today is stamped Is_External__c = true in the switch so it keeps the visibility it has now. Child and related records inherit the model, deliberately, so nothing leaks through the side.

The mechanism

A data-layer flag, not the record type, drives the sharing

Record type and the boolean are set together, but the sharing rule keys off the data-layer flag, not the record-type label. That is deliberate:

  • Record type is a UI/process construct — it drives layouts, picklists and processes. On its own it does not control who sees a record.
  • The flag is explicit and queryable — a criteria-based sharing rule, an integration, and a report can all read Is_External__c directly, and it does not silently break if someone reorders or re-purposes record types later.

So each record carries both: the record type for the experience, the boolean for the boundary. In practice they are set in lockstep, and either the boolean or the RecordTypeId could be the rule criterion — the boolean is preferred for the reasons above.

For the developersOWD on the recruitment objects goes to Private (today: Contact = Public Read/Write; the rebuild objects ATS_Job__c, Application__c, Interview__c, Placement__c are authored ReadWrite). One criteria-based sharing rule per object: Is_External__c = true → share to a "Spain ATS" public group, Read/Write — this reproduces today's open behaviour for external records. Internal records (flag false) fall through to the private baseline and are granted only by ownership + role hierarchy + explicit HR-group share. Backfill Is_External__c = true on all existing records before/at the flip so no current user loses visibility. This composes with the country-keyed rules from the rebuild's post-cutover privatisation stage — the two axes (country, internal/external) stack.

The internal side

Private, plus a hierarchy that rolls up and never sideways or upward

An internal record is owned by whoever creates it (a regional director, an HR recruiter). On the private baseline:

  • HR sees all internal processes — via a share to the HR public group (they are the cross-cutting function that must see everything internal).
  • Managers see down their own line only — the internal role hierarchy grants a parent role sight of records owned below it. A regional director sees their own and their reports'; they do not see a peer's, and a report does not see upward.
  • Nobody outside the line sees it at all — no external-population share matches an internal record, so it is invisible to the wider recruitment org by default.

This is exactly the "tree where those above see below, never the reverse" the requirement described — and it is delivered by native role hierarchy on a private object, no code required for the common case.

The trap

Periphery records must inherit the model

Confidentiality leaks through the weakest related record. It is not enough to privatise the candidate, job and application — the whole graph around an internal process has to carry the boundary:

  • Master-detail children inherit automatically — screening answers under a screening, for instance, follow the parent's sharing. These are safe by construction.
  • Lookup-related records do not — interviews, placements, activities, notes and attachments related by lookup need their own private OWD and their own rule, or an internal interview sits open while the internal process it belongs to is locked.

So the design includes an explicit pass over the object graph to classify each related object as inherit-by-master-detail or needs-its-own-rule. Mapping that graph in full is an open item, but the principle is fixed here: no related record is left open behind a private internal process.

Alternatives considered

Flag-driven, record-type-driven, or manual groups

Data-layer flag drives sharing (record type for UX)Chosen

Private baseline; a criteria-based rule on Is_External__c re-opens external records to everyone; internal stays private and rolls up the internal hierarchy. Explicit, queryable, integration-safe, and robust to later record-type changes. Cost: a data backfill on existing records and a periphery-object pass.

Record type alone drives visibilityRejected

Tempting because the record type already separates internal from external in the UI. Rejected: a record type does not control sharing by itself, and keying access to the UI construct couples two concerns that should move independently — reorganise record types and you silently reshape access.

Manual groups / queues per internal processRejected

Hand-share each internal process to a chosen set of people. Rejected: it does not scale, it has no automatic roll-up (a manager would have to be added by hand to every report's process), and it is error-prone in exactly the case where a mistake is a confidentiality breach.

This is the record-level design; the org-wide default and the cutover posture it sits on are the rebuild record's Security decision. Next: what actually gets built to deliver it.

Decision 2 · What we build for HR

This is a build, not just a sharing flip

Onboarding a team means building things: record types and the internal/external flag, a net-new internal role hierarchy, HR persona permission sets, and whatever onboarding-specific components the requirements turn out to need. All of it on the new custom model — never on the packaged product being retired.

Solution decision · draft for peer review · 16 July 2026

What this decides. The concrete components the HR rollout builds, distinct from the access rules on the previous page. The rename of this initiative to "HR Rollout and sharing-model refactor" is exactly because of this list — it is not only a privatisation stage.

The one rule over all of it. Every component is built on the new native model (ATS_Job__c, Application__c, and the rest), not on the TargetRecruit package. Building HR onto the package would be building on a platform already committed to retirement — instant throwaway work. This is the same doctrine the rebuild follows.

The build list

What gets created

Record types + flag

Internal and external record types on the recruitment objects, plus the Is_External__c boolean. The record types drive the internal layouts, picklists and process; the flag drives the sharing. Set together, used for different jobs. This is the seam the whole access model keys off.

Internal role hierarchy

A net-new internal sub-tree for the HR function. A manager over a team, rolling up so a leader sees their line's internal processes and no one sees upward — structurally like the recruiting branch, but separate and for internal work. Built from scratch, no users to migrate.

HR persona permission set

An HR access profile on the new objects. Object and field rights plus the app/tabs for the internal team — mirrored from the recruiter grant and scoped to internal work, following the same persona-permission-set method the access switch uses. Provisions licences and users.

HR public group + shares

The HR group and the sharing that grants it all internal records. The cross-cutting "HR sees everything internal" grant, plus the criteria-based external-population share that leaves external hiring open — both authored as part of this build.

Onboarding components

Whatever internal recruitment / onboarding needs that external hiring does not. Possibly a distinct kind of record, onboarding screens, or form pre-fill from existing systems. This is the least-defined part and depends on HR requirements — parked as an open question, not assumed.

Periphery coverage

Private OWD + rules on the lookup-related objects (interviews, placements, activities, notes, attachments) so an internal process does not leak through a related record. The graph pass that scopes this is an open item.

Reuse vs new

Reuse the recruitment objects, or give HR its own?

A genuine design question sits inside the build: do internal processes reuse the same Application__c / ATS_Job__c objects as external hiring (separated only by record type + flag), or do they get their own internal objects?

Reuse the recruitment objects Leaning

Internal work lives on the same objects, distinguished by record type and the internal/external flag.

  • One object model, one matching/search surface, one set of automations to maintain — internal work inherits the recruitment capability for free.
  • The flag-driven sharing already separates them cleanly, so a separate object buys little isolation the flag doesn't give.
  • Risk: if internal onboarding turns out to need a very different shape, record types stretch thin.

Separate internal objects Held

Dedicated internal-process objects, isolated from the external model.

  • Maximum isolation — internal data physically apart from external.
  • But it duplicates the object model, the automations and the matching surface, and the flag-based sharing already gives the isolation that matters.
  • Justified only if HR requirements diverge sharply from recruitment — which we don't yet know.

The lean is reuse-with-record-types, because the access design already isolates internal work and reuse avoids duplicating the whole model. But this is explicitly held pending HR requirements — if onboarding is structurally unlike external hiring, the answer changes. Recorded as an open question.

Decision 3 · When it lands

At the cutover, or after it

One business decision governs the whole sequencing, because HR onboarding and privatisation are inseparable. Onboard HR after the cutover and day one stays open and clean; onboard at the cutover and the private model must be born on day one. The recommendation is after — the People lead owns the call.

Solution decision · draft for peer review · 16 July 2026

What this decides. Whether the HR rollout and the private access model land at the platform cutover or as a stage after it. It is a single fork because you cannot onboard HR onto an open model — the moment confidential internal processes exist, "everyone sees everything" is a live breach. So wherever HR onboarding lands, privatisation lands with it.

What we recommend. Onboard HR after the cutover. The cutover then moves no access boundary, and the HR build plus privatisation run as their own rehearsable, roll-back-able stage on quiet timing. Because HR has no existing users or data, deferring costs nothing in rework. Go to born-private-at-cutover only if the People lead declares HR cannot wait.

The coupling

HR onboarding date equals privatisation date

The rebuild's security decision defaults to carrying today's open model forward at cutover and privatising later, precisely because privacy alone has no forcing deadline. HR breaks that assumption: its processes are confidential from the first record, so the open interim that is harmless for external data is unacceptable for internal data. The two dates lock together:

The rule

Privatisation cannot land before HR onboarding (nothing to protect yet) and cannot land after it (confidential data would sit exposed in between). So the privatisation date is the HR onboarding date. The cutover is simply the earliest that date can be.

The fork

After the cutover, or at it

Onboard HR after the cutover Recommended

Cutover carries today's open model forward unchanged; the HR build + privatisation run afterwards as their own stage.

  • The riskiest window stays clean — no OWD flip, no country key, no new role tree, no HR persona sets crammed into go-live.
  • The HR build + privatisation get their own rehearsal and their own rollback, on empty-of-risk timing.
  • Costs nothing to defer: HR has no existing users or data, so there is no rework in standing them up a few weeks later.

Worst case The stage slips and HR waits longer than hoped — visible and recoverable, and the external world is unaffected throughout.

Onboard HR at the cutover Conditional

The private model is born on day one — the only option if HR must go live with the switch.

  • HR is productive from go-live, with no interim wait.
  • But the private model, country key, sharing rules and internal role tree all land in the cutover — the worst window to add complexity.
  • The access layer now carries cutover rollback risk (the default path has none), so a fail-back must unwind the privatisation too.

When it's right Only if the People lead declares HR's need genuinely immediate. Even then, prefer a lightweight temporary bridge over building on TargetRecruit.

Default to after-the-cutover; treat at-the-cutover as a live conditional branch, not a rejection. Both paths are designed and ready — the same record types, flag, internal role tree and persona sets are built either way; only the timing moves. The recommendation is after, to keep go-live clean and lose nothing. The born-private branch fires only on an explicit business decision, and even an urgent HR need is better bridged with a throwaway tool than by building the full model on the product being retired.

Who decides

This is the People lead's call

The technical answer is the same in both branches; what differs is urgency, and only the business can weigh that. The meeting named the person responsible for People as the decider. Until that call is made, the cutover plan carries the after-the-cutover default, and the born-private branch stays ready. The decision must be made before the cutover plan is locked, because it changes what lands in the go-live window.

What's not settled

Open questions — gather before we build

Requirements to gather

What we need from the HR team
